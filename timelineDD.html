In 1535 Fray Tomás de Berlanga was the first documented human to step foot on the Galápagos Islands. After Fray Tomas reported his travels to the king of Spain, the Galápagos Islands appeared on a Map for the first time. Around 1680 Whalers, Pirates, and Buccaneers began to stop at the Galápagos Islands during their travels. While on the islands, they hunted the tortoises to eat their meat. In 1832 the country of Ecuador sent General Jose Villamil to create a small human settlement and claim the Galápagos Islands for Ecuador. In 1835 Charles Darwin visited the Galápagos Islands during the voyage of the H. M. S. Beagle. During the trip he began to develop the theory of Natural Selection. In 1959 the government of the country of Ecuador created a law that made most of the Galápagos Islands the “Galápagos National Park” or the “Parque Nacional Galápagos” in Spanish. This law helps to better protect the Galápagos against harmful human impacts. Additionally, the new law banned the capture of species, such as iguanas and tortoises. In 1979 the Galápagos Islands were named a UNESCO (United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization) world heritage site.
